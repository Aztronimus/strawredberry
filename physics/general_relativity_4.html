Tensors (in matrix notation)

Basis change

Describing one basis in terms of the other \begin{align} e_i' &= m_{i,j}\ e_j\\ e_i &= m_{i,j}'\ e_j'\\ \end{align} Describing one vector in different basis \begin{align} v &= v_i\ e_i = v_i'\ e_i'\\ \end{align} \begin{align} v_i &= m_{j,i}'\ v_j'\\ v_i' &= m_{j,i}\ v_j\\ \end{align}

Scalar product with metric

Metric is how one basis perceive other basis, a basis always perceives itself as cartesian because the components of the basis points perpendicular to each other \begin{align} e_i &= \delta_i^j\ e_j\\ g_{i,j} &= e_i \cdot e_j = \delta_i^j\\ \end{align} But how does it perceives another basis? \begin{align} e_i' &= m_{i,j}\ e_j\\ g_{i,j} &= e_i' \cdot e_j' = m_{i,k}\ m_{j,k}\\ \end{align} Dot product with cartesian metric \begin{align} u \cdot v &= u_i\ \delta_i^j\ v_j = u_i\ v_i\\ \end{align} Dot product with a no-cartesian metric \begin{align} u \cdot v &= u_i\ g_{i,j}\ v_j\\ \end{align}

Dual basis

\begin{align} e_i \cdot e^j &= \delta_i^j \end{align} \begin{align} e^i &= m_{i,j}\ e_j\\ \end{align} \begin{align} e_i \cdot e^j = g_{i,k}\ m_{j, k} &= \delta_i^j\\ g^{h,i}\ g_{i,k}\ m_{j, k} &= g^{h,i}\ \delta_i^j\\ \delta_h^k\ m_{j, k} &= g^{h,i}\ \delta_i^j\\ m_{j, h} &= g^{h,j}\\ \end{align} \begin{align} e^i &= g^{j, i}\ e_j\\ \end{align} Describing the same vector in dual basis \begin{align} v &= v_i\ e^i = v^i\ e_i\\ &= v_i\ g^{j, i}\ e_j\\ v^i &= g^{i, j}\ v_j\\ \end{align}

New metric with new basis

\begin{align} a &= a_i\ e_i = a_i'\ e_i'\\ b &= b_i\ e_i = b_i'\ e_i'\\ \end{align} \begin{align} a \cdot b &= a_i\ g_{i,j}\ b_j\\ &= a_i'\ g_{i,j}'\ b_j'\\ &= (m_{h,i}'\ a_h')\ g_{i,j}\ (m_{k,j}'\ b_k')\\ &= a_h'\ (m_{h,i}'\ g_{i,j}\ m_{k,j}')\ b_k'\\ \end{align} \begin{align} g_{i,j}' &= m_{h,i}'\ g_{i,j}\ m_{k,j}'\\ \end{align}
