Space-time

Space-time transformations

Not rigorous description of the space time transformations.

Space-time is the way an spectator describes events from its own point of view, if something happens, it is necessary to say where and when, specifiying the position and the time, if space and time are components of a point, and the bidimensional graphic has the axis of space and time, then events are points in the space time of the observer.

The space time for one observer it is not always the same space time of another observer, it can be shifted in space and desynchronized in time, but these two differences are not relevant when measuring the distance between events, there is however a third difference: when the observer is moving at a constant speed relative to another, all the coordinate systems have their own basis vectors that describes the position of all the points on it, but how to transform the basis vectors of one coordinate system to another?, that is a transformation, and the transformation we need to change the basis vectors of spectator that is not moving to the basis vectors of another perspective that is moving is called a space-time transformation.

Space-time rules

Again, this explanation is not rigorous.

  • One to one

    This means that each one of all the events in the space-time of one observer corresponds one and only one event for any other observer.

  • No invertion

    If in the same place one observer sees one event occurring before other, after the transformation this must remain equal to preserve causality, avoiding the invertion of cause-effect to effect-cause, this is not only a rule for time, it also applies for space, but not that rigorous, in the same time something that is in the left of an object for one observer cannot be at the right after the transformation (supposing that the two observers started with the same space-time before start moving relative each other)

Quotes notation

From your perspective, you are not moving
All the coordinates you measure are not quoted \begin{align} t, x, y, z, \cdots, v, r, \cdots \end{align} The coordinates the moving perspective measure are quoted \begin{align} t', x', y', z', \cdots, v', r', \cdots \end{align}

Galilean transformation

If the moving perspective moves only in his own time, that leads it to move in your time but also in your space \(vt\) steps (if \(t = 1\), then \(vt = v\)).
In this transformation events that are simultaneous for one oberserver, are simultaneous for any observer that travels at any speed.
\(ct\) is the distance traveled by a ray of light in a given time \(t\) \begin{align} (\begin{array}{cc} ct' & 0 \end{array}) \mathbf B' &= (\begin{array}{cc} ct & x \end{array}) \mathbf B\\ \\ t &= t'\\ x &= vt\\ \\ (\begin{array}{cc} ct & 0 \end{array}) \mathbf B' &= (\begin{array}{cc} ct & vt \end{array}) \mathbf B\\ (\begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \end{array}) \mathbf B' &= \left(\begin{array}{cc} 1 & \Large \frac{v}{c} \end{array}\right) \mathbf B \end{align} Lastly, in the moving perspective, taking steps in space don't makes it to move in your time
The distance of moving one step in space for an observer, is equivalent to the distance for all the observers. \begin{align} (\begin{array}{cc} 0 & x' \end{array}) \mathbf B' &= (\begin{array}{cc} ct & x \end{array}) \mathbf B\\ \\ t &= 0\\ x &= x'\\ \\ (\begin{array}{cc} 0 & x \end{array}) \mathbf B' &= \left(\begin{array}{cc} 0 & x \end{array}\right) \mathbf B\\ (\begin{array}{cc} 0 & 1 \end{array}) \mathbf B' &= (\begin{array}{cc} 0 & 1 \end{array}) \mathbf B \end{align} The transformation in matrix notation is as follows: \begin{align} B' &= TB & B &= T^{-1}B'\\ \left(\begin{array}{c} c\mathbf t'\\ \mathbf x' \end{array}\right) &= \left(\begin{array}{c} 1 & \Large \frac{v}{c}\\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right) \left(\begin{array}{c} c\mathbf t\\ \mathbf x \end{array}\right)& \left(\begin{array}{c} c\mathbf t\\ \mathbf x \end{array}\right) &= \left(\begin{array}{c} 1 & -\Large \frac{v}{c}\\ 0 & 1 \end{array}\right) \left(\begin{array}{c} c\mathbf t'\\ \mathbf x' \end{array}\right) \end{align} Transformation of the components of a vector described in both basis \begin{align} V' &= \left(T^{-1}\right)^TV & V &= T^T{V'}\\ \left(\begin{array}{c} V_t'\\ V_x' \end{array}\right) &= \left(\begin{array}{c} 1 & 0\\ -\Large \frac{v}{c} & 1 \end{array}\right) \left(\begin{array}{c} V_t\\ V_x \end{array}\right)& \left(\begin{array}{c} V_t\\ V_x \end{array}\right) &= \left(\begin{array}{c} 1 & 0\\ \Large \frac{v}{c} & 1 \end{array}\right) \left(\begin{array}{c} V_t'\\ V_x' \end{array}\right) \end{align}

Lorentz factor

Statements:

  • An object will continue moving in a straight line unless it is acted on by an unbalanced force.
  • Two observers moving towards each other at a constant speed will measure the same speed at which the other observer is approaching or moving away.
  • The speed of light is constant regardless the observer.
  • Traveling parallel to a pair of light beams that are also traveling parallel to each other will not change the distance between them, regardless of the speed of the observer.

Definitions:

  • \(r\) is the displacement covered by the ray of light measured by the stationary perspective when the ray reaches a certain height \(y\).
  • \(r'\) is the displacement covered by the ray of light measured from the moving perspective when the ray reaches a certain height \(y'\).
  • \(y = y'\) to make the comparison between the time and distance measured by both observers.

The moving person shoots a ray of light in his \(y'\) direction.
Since the ray of light will stay above the moving person for both observers, the ray of light will describe an inclined path for the stationary perspective.
There are two events separated by space and time that both observers can measure: the first event is when the light beam is shot by the moving observer, the second event is when the light beam has reached the height \(y = y'\).

\begin{align} x &= vt& r &= ct& r' &= ct' \end{align} \begin{align} c &= \frac{r}{t} = \frac{r'}{t'}& \gamma &= \frac{r}{r'} = \frac{t}{t'} \end{align} \begin{align} \frac{x}{r} &= \frac{vt}{ct} = \frac{v}{c} \end{align} \begin{align} r^2 &= {r'}^2 + x^2& {r'}^2 &= r^2 - x^2& \end{align} \begin{align} \frac{{r'}^2}{r^2} &= 1 - \frac{x^2}{r^2}& \frac{r^2}{{r'}^2} &= \frac{1}{1 - {\Large \frac{x^2}{r^2}}} \end{align} \begin{align} \gamma &= \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - {\Large \frac{v^2}{c^2}}}} \end{align}

Lorentz transformation

According to special relativity, simultaneous events separated by a distance for you are not simultaneous for an observer that is moving at a non-zero constant speed.
For the moving perspective, taking one step in his time takes him to move not only in your time but also in your space
One unit of time passes for the moving perspective, knowing that we can get the time it passes for an static perspective \begin{align} (\begin{array}{cc} ct' & 0 \end{array}) \mathbf B' &= (\begin{array}{cc} ct & x \end{array}) \mathbf B\\ \\ t &= \gamma t'\\ x &= vt\\ \\ \left(\begin{array}{cc} {\Large \frac{ct}{\gamma}} & 0 \end{array}\right) \mathbf B' &= \left(\begin{array}{cc} ct & vt \end{array}\right) \mathbf B\\ (\begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \end{array}) \mathbf B' &= \gamma \left(\begin{array}{cc} 1 & {\Large \frac{v}{c}} \end{array}\right) \mathbf B\\ \end{align} Using the time it takes a ray of light to travel a unit of distance for the moving perspective we can measure that from a static perspective there's not only a difference in distance between the traveler and the ray of light, but also in time.
Time it takes a ray of light to travel a unit of distance (from moving's perspective) \begin{align} (\begin{array}{cc} 0 & x' \end{array}) \mathbf B' &= (\begin{array}{cc} ct & x \end{array}) \mathbf B\\ \\ t &= \frac{v}{c}\cdot\frac{x}{c}\\ x &= \gamma x'\\ \\ \left(\begin{array}{cc} 0 & {\Large \frac{x}{\gamma}} \end{array}\right) \mathbf B' &= \left(\begin{array}{cc} {\Large \frac{v}{c}}x & x \end{array}\right) \mathbf B\\ \left(\begin{array}{cc} 0 & 1 \end{array}\right) \mathbf B' &= \gamma \left(\begin{array}{cc} {\Large \frac{v}{c}} & 1 \end{array}\right) \mathbf B\\ \end{align} Transformation in matrix notation: \begin{align} \mathbf B' &= \mathbf T \mathbf B & \mathbf B &= \mathbf T^{-1} \mathbf B'\\ \left(\begin{array}{c} c\mathbf t'\\ \mathbf x' \end{array}\right) &= \gamma \left(\begin{array}{c} 1 & \Large \frac{v}{c}\\ \Large \frac{v}{c} & 1 \end{array}\right) \left(\begin{array}{c} c\mathbf t\\ \mathbf x \end{array}\right) & \left(\begin{array}{c} c\mathbf t\\ \mathbf x \end{array}\right) &= \gamma \left(\begin{array}{c} 1 & -\Large \frac{v}{c}\\ -\Large \frac{v}{c} & 1 \end{array}\right) \left(\begin{array}{c} c\mathbf t'\\ \mathbf x' \end{array}\right) \end{align} Transformation of the vector components: \begin{align} \mathbf V' &= \left(\mathbf T^{-1}\right)^T\mathbf V & \mathbf V &= \mathbf T^T\mathbf V'\\ \left(\begin{array}{c} V_t'\\ V_x' \end{array}\right) &= \gamma \left(\begin{array}{c} 1 & -\Large \frac{v}{c}\\ -\Large \frac{v}{c} & 1 \end{array}\right) \left(\begin{array}{c} V_t\\ V_x \end{array}\right)& \left(\begin{array}{c} V_t\\ V_x \end{array}\right) &= \gamma \left(\begin{array}{c} 1 & \Large \frac{v}{c}\\ \Large \frac{v}{c} & 1 \end{array}\right) \left(\begin{array}{c} V_t'\\ V_x' \end{array}\right) \end{align} Where did \(tc^2 = vx\) come from? \begin{align} ct &= \frac{c}{v}x \end{align} \begin{align} ct_a &= x - k_a& ct_b &= k_b - x\\ \\ t_a &= -t& t_b &= t\\ x &= vt_a& x &= vt_b\\ \\ k_a &= \left(1 - \frac{v}{c}\right)ct& k_b &= \left(1 + \frac{v}{c}\right)ct\\ \end{align} \begin{align} x_x &= \frac{1}{2}(k_b + k_a)& x_t &= \frac{1}{2}(k_b - k_a)\\ &= ct& &= \frac{v}{c}t\\ \end{align} Elapsed time between two events separated only by distance in \(S'\) frame of reference \begin{align} x_t &= \frac{v}{c} \cdot \frac{x_x}{c} \end{align}

Time and space dilation and contraction

Time dilation
Two events in \(S'\) separated only by time (not by space) are separated by time in \(S\) as follows: \begin{align} t &= t'\gamma \end{align} Length dilation
Two events in \(S'\) separated only by space (not by time) are separated by space in \(S\) as follows: \begin{align} x &= x'\gamma \end{align} Length contraction
Two objects separated by a space in \(S'\) are separated by space in \(S\)
We start with the time in \(S\) that separates two simultaneous events in \(S'\)
\begin{align} ct &= \frac{v}{c}x\\ \end{align} Then we use the trayectory of an object that moves at a constant speed... \begin{align} ct &= \frac{c}{v}x\\ \end{align} ... and modify it to become the trayectory of an object that passes through the coordinates of the event \((ct, x)\), where: \begin{align} ct &= \frac{c}{v}x - k& ct &= \frac{v}{c}x& x &= x'\gamma\\ \end{align} \begin{align} k &= \frac{c}{v} \cdot \frac{x'}{\gamma}\\ \end{align} The complete trayectory of that object is: \begin{align} ct &= \frac{c}{v}\left(x - \frac{x'}{\gamma}\right) \end{align} \(x\) is the distance of the same two objects at the same time in \(S\)
\(x'\) is the distance of two objects at the same time in \(S'\)
Now at the same time (\(ct = 0\)), the distance between objects is: \begin{align} x' &= x\gamma \end{align}
